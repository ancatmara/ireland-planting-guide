⚠️ Drainage: Your Most Important Rule

West of Ireland clay is heavy, poorly draining and mildly acidic to neutral (pH ~6.0–7.0). Improve drainage before planting every time.

  • Dig each hole at least twice the width and depth of the rootball
  • Mix excavated clay 50/50 with multipurpose compost
  • Add 20–30% horticultural grit by volume
  • Mulch with horticultural bark (5–7cm), keeping mulch away from stems/crowns
  • Test your soil pH before planting — west of Ireland soils vary considerably

Reference photoHydrangea macrophylla — view on Wikipedia
Light🌥️ Partial shade preferred. Morning sun with afternoon shade is ideal. Bigleaf hydrangea performs best in areas with dappled sunlight, partial shade, or deep shade. Protect it from afternoon sun. The macrophylla cultivars famously tolerate coastal exposure.
Soil needsThey love a fertile, humus-rich, clay soil. pH 5.0–6.0 for blue flowers; 6.0–7.0 for pink flowers. Moist but well-drained. Good salt tolerance. Flower colour changes depending on soil pH — blue in acid soils (high aluminium availability), mauve in lightly acid to neutral soil, pink in alkaline conditions.
Soil prepBefore planting, dig in well-rotted compost or manure to the whole area (not just the planting hole), using a bucketful per square yard/metre. Make the planting hole as deep as the root ball and three times wider. For blue flowers: use ericaceous compost in the planting hole and grow in ericaceous compost in containers.
Planting depthAt pot level; place the top of the rootball at or slightly above surrounding soil level. Shallow-rooted — avoid hoeing around it as roots can be easily damaged.
Spacing90cm–2.4m between (check label for variety size)
FertiliserFertilise once in spring with a fertiliser designed to encourage blooms such as one high in phosphorus (e.g. 15-30-15). For blue flowers: feed with a fertiliser low in phosphorus and high in potassium and use ericaceous compost. If you're gardening on alkaline soils and want to retain blue flowers, grow in containers using ericaceous compost. Stop feeding after August.
Warning ⚠️⚠️ Pruning note: Hydrangeas that flower on old wood should not be pruned hard as you will lose this summer's flowers. Prune in mid-spring; only remove the withered flowerheads and dead parts. Traditionally leave old flowers on through winter to protect new growth from cold.
CompanionsAstilbe, hostas, ferns (all love similar moist, semi-shaded conditions). Yarrow pairs beautifully with hydrangea. Heucheras, hardy geraniums, and hellebores as underplanting. Behind low-growing perennials such as bergenia at the border front.
Pot sizeStandard mophead varieties reach 1–1.5m in containers and need 50–60cm pots at maturity. Compact cultivars like Cityline Paris manage in 30–35cm pots. Use ericaceous compost for blue flowers; standard John Innes No.3 (2 parts) + multipurpose compost (1 part) for pink flowers.
West of IrelandHydrangeas are one of the quintessential plants of Irish gardens. Macrophylla hydrangeas tolerate coastal exposure too. The mild, moist Atlantic climate suits them perfectly. The west of Ireland's naturally slightly acidic soils tend to produce beautiful blue-mauve flowers. Choose a sheltered spot away from frost pockets — late frosts can damage new growth.
